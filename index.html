CASE_01 AI Agent Platform 14 months · Lead PM

An AI agent platform that other teams trust to run their products on.

Enterprise customers were drowning in high-volume, judgment-intensive tasks: document classification, exception triage, support routing. We built the platform that automated 84% of that load.

The problem

Twelve enterprise customers had the same pain shape — too many decisions, not enough humans. Engineering wanted to ship something fast; customer-success wanted something reliable; founders wanted something they could trust to run unattended. The hard part wasn't picking a model. It was building the platform around it: prompt versioning, evaluation harnesses, retrieval-augmented generation, fallback flows, and the kind of observability that lets you ship without crossing your fingers.

My approach

I shipped user-facing and API products on the same stack — same evaluation harness, same logging, same idempotency contracts. Deeply understood user needs through 40+ founder-style discovery interviews. Drove product development by setting the pace, prioritizing ruthlessly, and unblocking execution. The platform serves both internal teams who consume the API and end-users who see the agent's output — and I designed surprisingly great details into both.

CASE_02 Payments Infrastructure 14 months · Lead PM

Cutting a payment error rate from 12% to 1.4%.

A top-5 US bank's commission-payout engine was producing 12% error rates — quietly costing $2M+ a quarter in reconciliation work and eroding agent trust.

The problem

Six teams, one rule engine, no shared source of truth. Payment logic lived across three systems with subtle inconsistencies. Each month-end produced a backlog of disputes, reconciliations, and one-off corrections. Engineering wanted a rewrite; Operations wanted stability; Finance wanted the errors to stop yesterday. My job: design a path through that respected all three, without breaking idempotency, audit trails, or settlement timing.

My approach

Four-week discovery. Shadowed 12 commission analysts. Mapped rule logic across all three systems. Sequenced the program into three releases: stabilize disputed rules first, migrate to a single event-driven engine second, deprecate legacy paths last. Partnered with engineering on the architecture: AWS API Gateway as the edge, Lambda for rule evaluation, EventBridge for state transitions, SQS for retries with idempotency keys.

CASE_03 Regulated · Audit-Visible 7 months · Lead PM

Standing up a platform with zero audit findings.

A new fraud-decisioning platform at FINRA spanning 6 business lines, with audit visibility from week one and 12 customer-driven initiatives to ship simultaneously.

The problem

New initiative, no historical baseline, audit visibility from day one. Six business lines with conflicting priorities. The path forward wasn't unclear because the work was hard — it was unclear because too many stakeholders were being polite about what they actually needed. Fraud-decisioning at scale lives or dies on signal quality, and nobody had written down what "signal" meant for this product.

My approach

Synthesis sprint: 40+ user interviews, 4 surveys, 8 working sessions. Forced trade-offs by sequencing initiatives against a single weighted scoring model. Authored a 12-month roadmap and validated it line-by-line with each business-line lead. Built a risk register with named owners and weekly review cadence. Threat-modeled the AI risk-scoring system itself — adversarial inputs, model evasion, false-positive cost to legitimate users.

03 / How I work

Six things I've learned building for builders.

i.

The boring parts are the product

Onboarding flows. Empty states. Error messages. The bits founders see when something goes wrong. I obsess over these because every "surprisingly great detail" lives in there.

ii.

Be a peer to engineering

If I can't reason about distributed systems, idempotency, and exception handling in the same room as the engineers, I'm not actually doing technical PM work. I keep my technical depth sharp as a deliberate practice.

iii.

2x better, not 10% better

The AI enthusiast in me is allergic to incrementalism. If a new tool, model, or workflow doesn't promise a 2x improvement, I'm probably not going to upend my routine for it. The corollary: when something is 2x better, I switch immediately.

iv.

Set the pace. Then keep it

I drive product development by setting the pace, prioritizing ruthlessly, and unblocking execution. The team's velocity is my responsibility, not just the roadmap.

v.

Whatever it takes

Persistent operator. If the team needs me to write the docs, talk to support, debug a customer escalation at 9pm, or open a Notion page and start typing the strategy — that's what I do. Job titles are descriptions, not boundaries.

vi.

Measure user impact, precisely

I'm adept with data and skeptical of metrics that don't tie back to a real user behavior. The KPI I trust most is the one a customer would describe in their own words.
